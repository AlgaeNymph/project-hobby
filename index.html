How to write, and doing so well.

My whole life I’ve always wanted to play god, but how can I do that when I’m a squishy mortal? From my observations, the easiest way of creating worlds is by imagining them into existence, something we’ve certainly been doing since oral mythology. However, if we want our worlds to last we need a more permanent medium than complex memes. That is essentially what writing is, and why it’s a hobby of mine. With writing, anyone with the basest of tools can be divinely empowered to create reality as they feel it should be.

Something to write with and on are the bare minimums, but if you’re reading this you almost certainly have a text editor. Windows machines come with WordPad, while Macs come with TextEdit. At the very least you’ll want one that automatically checks your spelling, meaning Windows users will have to download one. Thankfully, a search for ‘free windows text editor’ provides many options, both downloadable and online. That’s really it as far as what you physically need for writing, but doesn’t begin to cover writing well.

To write fiction well you’ll need to not only know the rules of your language but how to create an entertaining story. Reams worth of words have been written on these subjects, but I can thankfully connect you to those who can teach writing better than I can. So as not to overwhelm you I’ll start with the best three. The Purdue Online Writing Lab will help avoid both the worst blunders and the ire of English-majors turned editors. Springhole offers organized and comprehensive advice by topic, and even random generators to help with creativity. Lastly, Wordcounter not only provides a word count of what you pasted but also a detailed analysis, including the reading level.
